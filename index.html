Opening note – reflection

Your role as a manager

Considering the scenarios above, now think about the following question:

In your role as a manager, how much of your success depends on the following conditions:
• risks being identified and addressed early
• new ideas and solutions being developed
• staff being protected from hazards that can affect their wellbeing and performance.

A psychologically healthy and safe workplace is essential to creating these conditions and, as a manager, you have a key role and responsibility in creating such an environment.

Psychological health and safety

Two core components

Psychological health and safety has two core components: psychological safety and psychosocial hazard management.

Understanding these components and your role in implementing each allows you to take practical steps to meet your responsibilities and support a healthy and safe workplace.

Psychological safety

Speaking up with respect and support

Psychological safety is a shared belief that team members can openly raise ideas, questions and concerns and be met with respect and support. It involves feeling confident that one will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up.

It doesn’t mean lowering standards, being overly ‘nice’, or avoiding challenging conversations. The aim is to achieve a learning zone, where both psychological safety and accountability are high.

In healthcare, psychological safety supports early identification of risks, sharing improvement ideas and enables delivery of high-quality, safe care.

Psychological safety is essential to living the NSW Health CORE values in practice. As a manager, you share responsibility for fostering psychological safety through active engagement with your team and collaboration with other managers. Topic two of this module provides practical guidance and resources to help you foster a psychologically safe workplace.

Managing psychosocial hazards

Identifying and controlling work factors

By managing psychosocial hazards, you protect your staff through identifying and controlling psychosocial work factors that can cause harm. This supports better work experiences and performance, and reduces disruption and costs linked to incidents.

Under NSW Work Health and Safety (WHS) law, you are responsible for ensuring these risks are eliminated or minimised so far as is reasonably practicable.

Topic three provides clear steps on how to manage psychosocial hazards. Topic four focuses on how to respond appropriately if an incident occurs.

Conclusion and outcome

Leading both together

When you lead psychological safety and manage psychosocial hazards together, you build a workplace that is psychologically healthy and safe. You bring the NSW Health CORE values to life and meet your organisational and legal responsibilities.

Staff feel more comfortable speaking up, new ideas are shared, and risks are reduced. They feel supported and protected at work, and work experiences and performance improve.

This module gives you the practical guidance and tools to create a psychologically healthy and safe workplace.
